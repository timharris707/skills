Round 1 Proceed with care

I'm the first‑principles & economics seat. I worked the numbers in the document line by line. Short version: the arithmetic is clean, but two of the three load‑bearing assumptions are economically inconsistent with the budget, and both errors bias the decision toward "take it."

1. Verdict

CAUTION — confidence: medium. The year‑one cash‑flow table is internally correct, but the case rests on (a) a "slightly higher take‑home" that is fragile to the exact state tax, (b) a childcare line that contradicts the one‑income premise, and (c) a year‑two "flips strongly positive" that is overstated by ~2×. Proceed only with the changes in §3.

What would change it to ship: a real apartment listing confirming an adequate family home at ≤ $4,100, plus a concrete, evidenced spouse re‑employment plan (live pipeline, target ≥ $50k), and a redone year‑two model at marginal tax with working‑parent childcare that still clears a meaningful margin.

2. Strongest objections

A. The childcare line is inconsistent with the "$0 spouse" premise — and it hides the real year‑two cost.

Year one assumes the spouse "earns $0 the first year" and is home job‑hunting — yet childcare is budgeted at $1,350/mo, higher than today's $1,150. If a parent is home all year, school‑age‑plus‑young kids need far less paid care, so year‑one savings are likely better than the stated $537. The mirror problem is worse: the moment the spouse works (the entire upside case), you lose "our free family childcare" with no family backup in the new city, and real HCOL care for two kids runs well above $1,350. The budget holds childcare flat regardless of the spouse's employment state, which is the one variable that drives it. Net effect: year one looks slightly too grim, year two looks much too rosy — and the bias points toward "go."

B. The year‑two "flips strongly positive" is overstated on two independent counts.

The claim: a $42,000 job makes "household take‑home rise roughly +$2,590/month." That's $31,080/yr net on $42k gross — 74% retention on income stacked on top of $176k in a high‑tax state. The marginal rate on that second income is ~35–40% (fed + high state + FICA), so realistic net is ~$2,100–2,300/mo, not $2,590 — an overstatement of ~$300–450/mo. Then subtract the new working‑parent childcare from objection A. The honest year‑two improvement is roughly half the advertised swing. The picture still turns positive, but "strongly" is doing unearned work.

C. "Take‑home slightly higher than now" is true only at the optimistic edge of the tax assumption.

You're claiming $176k single‑earner in a high‑tax coastal state nets $10,267/mo (30.0% effective deduction) vs today's $9,990 (26.0%). That's plausible but optimistic: push the effective rate to ~32% (very reachable in CA/NY‑class states on a concentrated single income) and take‑home falls to ~$9,970 — flat to slightly down, which erases the "raise more than offsets" framing entirely. The headline rests on a 1–2 point tax estimate.

D. The raise is nominal, not real. Frame this as buying an option, not getting a raise.

The "+37.5% raise" is a single‑line gross figure. On a household, COL‑adjusted basis, purchasing power falls in year one — the entire $48k raise is consumed by rent (+$1,900/mo), the lost $34k income, and higher tax. What you're actually buying is a call option on a higher lifetime earnings trajectory, priced at ~$2,213/mo (~$26.5k/yr) of foregone savings plus relocation plus the spouse's career disruption plus tail risk. That reframe is the correct lens — and it makes the decision turn on the durability of the trajectory claim and spouse re‑employment, not on the year‑one budget at all.

E. The sign‑on bonus is pre‑tax; it does not actually cover the move.

"A flat $20,000 sign‑on… the bonus roughly covers the move" against a $18,000 estimate. Sign‑on bonuses are taxable (supplemental ~22% fed + FICA + state withholding), so $20k gross nets ~$13–14k. There's a ~$4–5k hole that comes straight out of the $70k savings — before any moving‑cost overrun, and moves routinely overrun.

3. Recommended execution sequence

  1. Reprice the move and the bonus net‑of‑tax. Assume the $20k sign‑on nets ~$13–14k; budget the move at $18k + 15% contingency; fund the gap from savings explicitly.
  2. Verify the rent line with real listings for an adequate family home (3BR, tolerable commute) in the actual target neighborhoods. This single number (+$1,900/mo) dominates everything; at $4,600 the year‑one savings go negative.
  3. Rebuild the budget in two correct states, not one: (i) spouse‑home year one → childcare near‑zero; (ii) spouse‑working → childcare at real HCOL working‑parent rates and income at marginal tax. Decide on the honest year‑two number.
  4. Negotiate the offer before signing — this is the highest‑leverage step (see §4): larger/guaranteed relocation, COL adjustment, or a spouse‑landing/job‑search support clause.
  5. Set explicit kill criteria (e.g., "spouse not re‑employed at ≥ $X by month 14 → revisit").
  6. Only then decide. The year‑one budget is survivable; the decision is really a yes/no on the option in §2‑D.

4. Invariants and guardrails

  • 6‑month HCOL emergency fund preserved. At ~$9,730/mo expenses on one income with no family backup, keep ≥ ~$58k liquid after the move. The plan leaves ~$65k post‑move and adds only ~$6.4k/yr at $537/mo — thin. Do not let the move + bonus tax gap drop you below the floor.
  • Rent ceiling as a go/no‑go: if a verified adequate home exceeds ~$4,300/mo, the year‑one math breaks; renegotiate or walk.
  • Don't sign without testing the relocation package. A flat $20k for a cross‑country family move from a "big‑name employer" is below market; ask for net‑of‑tax relo, temporary housing, or a spouse job‑search benefit. The downside of asking is near zero.
  • Spouse re‑employment is a guardrail, not a footnote: require a concrete plan (target role/comp, pipeline, timeline) before moving, and a defined fallback if it slips.

5. Risks, stale assumptions, missing evidence

  • Single‑career concentration risk. The household sheds its second income and the safety of two earners to bet entirely on one new role at a new employer with no local network. New role + big org + no network = real first‑year fit/layoff risk; runway then is ~6–7 months in an expensive city far from family. The plan is cash‑positive but tail‑fragile.
  • Spouse opportunity cost is undercounted. It's not one year of $34k — it's the risk of prolonged underemployment plus loss of an established position. This is the least‑scrutinized and most decision‑relevant variable.
  • Identical lines that probably shouldn't be: "Two cars… $940," health premiums $320, and misc $390 are held constant across metros. A dense coastal metro may make one car redundant (savings) or make parking/insurance/tolls higher; HCOL misc/clothing typically rises. Every other line was COL‑adjusted; these weren't.
  • Rent escalation asymmetry: HCOL rents reset upward annually while the salary is fixed; the "year‑two flip" silently assumes rent holds at $4,100.
  • 401(k) ambiguity: if the $10,267 take‑home is already net of retirement contributions, the "$537/mo savings" understates real wealth accumulation — clarify, because it changes how tight year one truly is.
  • Missing evidence: an actual rent comp; the offer's full comp (equity/RSUs, bonus target, 401k match — none mentioned, and they can dominate base); state of destination (drives the tax estimate); the spouse's concrete re‑employment pipeline.

  • "+37.5% raise… monthly savings falling from $2,750 to $537… almost entirely because rent jumps +$1,900/month" — the document already concedes the raise is consumed; objection D just names what you're actually buying.

7. What I'd ask the other seats to challenge

  • The career/trajectory seat: quantify the option. Is the $128k→$176k base step durable and compounding, or a one‑time level shift? Estimate the PV of the trajectory bump — that single number decides whether paying ~$26.5k/yr + spouse disruption + tail risk is rational.

  • The negotiation seat: how much of this is fixable at the table (net‑of‑tax relo, COL adjustment, spouse job‑search benefit, sign‑on uplift) before any of the above math even matters?

Bottom line from the economics seat: the budget is honest arithmetic on optimistic assumptions.
