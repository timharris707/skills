**3. Strongest Remaining Objections**

The year-two upside depends on an unverified spouse job, but that job also creates new childcare costs and will be taxed at the household’s marginal rate.

The relocation package is underfunded. A `$20,000` gross sign-on does not cover an `$18,000` move once taxes, deposits, setup costs, and overruns are included.

The career upside may be worth buying, but it is still only asserted. They need evidence that the title, employer brand, manager, team stability, promotion path, and future marketability make the first-year risk rational.

**4. Recommended Execution Sequence**

1. Before acceptance, get the full written offer: base, bonus, equity/RSUs, 401(k), benefits, payroll timing, sign-on clawback, severance, start date, remote/hybrid expectations, travel load, and relocation terms.
2. Negotiate: company-paid movers or grossed-up relocation, 60-90 days temporary housing, lease/deposit support, sign-on clawback waived if laid off, delayed start or remote ramp, spouse job-search support, and possibly salary/equity if market comps support it.
3. Rebuild the model with actual payroll/tax/benefit numbers and net sign-on cash.
4. Verify the new-city budget with real data: 10 rental comps, school zones, aftercare availability, summer care, backup care, parking, tolls, insurance, utilities, and travel back to family.
5. Build the spouse plan before moving: target roles, pay range, licensing needs, recruiter calls, applications, schedule fit, and 90/180/270-day checkpoints.
6. If accepted, stage the move if possible: earner starts first or remotely, family follows only after lease, school, and childcare are locked.
7. Set kill criteria: if rent exceeds cap, cash floor breaks, role stability looks weak, or spouse pipeline is not credible by a fixed date, do not move or renegotiate.
